## Chapter 2: Understanding the Function Numbers

The most confusing part for many new users is the mysterious `function_num` argument, which controls how SUBTOTAL behaves. ## Chapter 1: Why AGGREGATE Exists

Imagine working in a large spreadsheet filled with real-world data: some rows are hidden, some cells contain errors such as `#DIV/0!` or `#N/A`, and you still need reliable totals, averages, or rankings. It is like a â€œsmart summaryâ€ tool that understands that not every cell in your range deserves a vote in the final result. [exceljet](https://exceljet.net/functions/aggregate-function)

Technically, AGGREGATE returns an â€œaggregate calculation,â€ which can be SUM, AVERAGE, MAX, MIN, COUNT, LARGE, SMALL, and many more, depending on the function number you choose. While SUBTOTAL offers 11 operations, AGGREGATE expands this to 19, adding useful options such as MEDIAN, MODE.SNGL, PERCENTILE.INC, QUARTILE.INC, LARGE, and SMALL. A simple story shows its usefulness. Suppose a column of numbers in E2:E6 includes one cell with `#N/A` because a lookup failed. If you use `=SUM(E2:E6)`, the result is `#N/A`, and your report falls apart. If instead you use `=AGGREGATE(9, 6, E2:E6)`, AGGREGATE uses function number 9 (SUM) with option 6 (ignore error values), quietly skipping the bad cell and returning a clean total. ## Chapter 2: Getting Comfortable with the Syntax

AGGREGATE comes in two forms, but both share the same basic structure: \(=AGGREGATE(function\_num, options, ref1, [ref2], ...)\) in reference mode, or \(=AGGREGATE(function\_num, options, array, [k])\) when using array-like calculations such as LARGE or SMALL. The first argument, `function_num`, selects what kind of aggregate you wantâ€”SUM, AVERAGE, MAX, etc.â€”using numbers from 1 to 19. The second argument, `options`, controls what AGGREGATE should ignore: nothing, hidden rows, error values, or a combination of these plus nested SUBTOTAL and AGGREGATE functions. The third argument is typically your data range (`ref1` or `array`), and the optional fourth argument `k` is used for functions that need an extra parameter, such as the k-th largest value. The function numbers are organized so that 1 means AVERAGE, 2 means COUNT, 3 is COUNTA, 4 is MAX, 5 is MIN, 6 is PRODUCT, 7 and 8 are standard deviations, 9 is SUM, 10 and 11 are variances, and higher numbers like 14 and 15 give access to LARGE and SMALL. Once you remember a handful of common codesâ€”such as 9 for SUM and 4 for MAXâ€”the rest of the syntax becomes surprisingly natural. The `options` argument deserves special attention because it drives much of AGGREGATEâ€™s power. ## Chapter 5: Choosing Between SUBTOTAL and AGGREGATE, and Best Practices

If you already know the SUBTOTAL function, AGGREGATE can feel like its more versatile sibling.
